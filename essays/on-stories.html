Personal Essay · 2023

On Stories

I look out my window and imagine the concrete, the entire world falling away, fading into what could have been millions of years ago. I see the many different predators that could snatch my throne in the sky, some with long necks, some with long wingspans. Fast forward many millions of years and I can see swamps, vast fields of grass, and Aristotle looking at the sky thinking, "I object to this being flat." Nineteen hundred years later, Shakespeare writes phrases so perpetual they stain the sky red. The world is discovered or rather it is devoured, with stories lighting up paths to newer lands. The sun, moon, stars, and elements all come into play. Countless inventions made, and countless countries ruined. I witness wars wax and wane. Islands rise, then drown. Storms, cyclones, tsunamis, and earthquakes shake the view from my window. Yet, stories thrive through it all. Stories spin into atoms, expanding and reacting, bursting into primal colors and forming the first fractured realities. I have a front-row seat as they bring life to the world. Stories make us novel in the never-ending cycle of evolution; they precipitate humans from other species. It is important to me to write our stories, to reach out to the stories of the past, find stitches that ripped, take elements from this world, and spin them into another. Stories have designed our reality and our universe, and I want to be one of its architects.

When I was eight, I sat on a stoop and breathed in. When the breath left me, it was ink on paper. I am more, the world is more when I write. Eventually, I shared my haphazard lines, and they felt infinite. Then, I discovered reading. This combination became the catalyst for my writing. It snuck in unsure, mild, and innocent, and grew up with me, diffusing through my limbs and materializing my atoms. I look at theories and formulae and see stories, stories evolving since time began. Our human requisite to communicate has led us from carved walls to myriad dialects, and this history departs our lips even today. We survive through these stories, myths, and legends woven with chaos.

The English language astounds me. We are at a moment in time when words exist, and we perceive them and stitch them into our pattern. "The circle of the English language has a well-defined centre but no discernible circumference." — Sir James Murray. I want to learn, grow, and think back on a year ago to see how different I was. Sir James Murray provided the world with the dictionary, the ultimate tool for writing. I want knowledge at the cusp of my fingertips, so I may transmute it into fantastical words that delve into the heart.
